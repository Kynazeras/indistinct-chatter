Jeymes Samuel's stylish debut film _The Harder They Fall_ is a western with a big casted to highly talented actors. The film opened in (some) cinemas recently and also dropped on Netflix. The story follows fictionalized versions of several famous African American cowboys from the 1800s in a tale of crime and revenge in the old west. The film has an eye-popping visual style and a star-studded sound track to match its cast.

**Quick Disclaimer:** I am not familiar with the real life people that are being portrayed in this movie. This review will serve as my critical reaction to the movie and not an analysis of its accuracy to the time period. There are several critics who have tackled the historical context and I doubt my voice would be able to disect it any better. I do want to read up on these people in the future because the seem supremely interesting.

In a brief but effective opening scene, we see a young Nat Love (Jonathan Majors) terrorized by the outlaw Rufus Buck (Idris Elba). Love's parents are killed and he is marked with a cross carved into his forehead. Not long after we get a major time skip in which the now adult Nat Love gets to exact revenge on one of Buck's assistants. Love abominates Buck and is on a warpath to take down criminals across the West. When he finds out Buck is a free man after years of being locked up, he is ready to put an end to Buck's treachery once and for all.

On his way to solve the problem, we meet a supporting cast full of interesting performers. Bill Pickett and Jim Beckworth (played by the duo of Edi Gathegi and RJ Cyler) make for great comedic relief as well as one of the most important running threads in the script. Cuffee (Danielle Deadwyler) gets to be an underestimated security guard who can bark just as hard as she bites. Bass Reeves (Delroy Lindo) is a sheriff who reluctantly joins one band of criminals to stop another. To top it off, Zazie Beetz gets to play the tough and charismatic Stagecoach Mary.

Rufus Buck's squad is no slouch when it comes to big name performers either. Helmed by a brooding Idris Elba, the team also boasts a magnetic performance from Regina King as Trudy Smith and Lakeith Stanfield as the backstabbing (literally) Cherokee Bill. Let's be honest, when have any of those performers let us down?

The movie has been criticized for being more style than substance and overlong. While I agree with the latter, the former is by design. If a filmmaker knows they want a movie to _look_ and _feel_ a certain way, then by all means they should go for it. Netflix has so many movies that have neither style or substance so seeing a film in their catalogue solidly nail its aesthetic choices is refreshing. And boy does it **nail** that look.

Each of the locations has a distinct color palette and layout. The buildings are generally devoid of imperfections. Paint is crisp and there is very little wear. The sets are eye-popping instead of realistic. They are the backdrops for the gorgeous paintings of each scene. The saloon bustles with warm tones as the crowd drinks and sings together. Redwood is clean and unharmed until the characters in this film ride through. Marysville is a town entirely made up of white people and it is **solid** white. That's not corny, it's cool.

The gunfights in the latter half of the movie move with the energy of a director channeling some of the greats (there are obvious Tarantino parallels) but also finding their own footing. There is a particular scene where Nat Love and Stagecoach Mary are fighting a crowd while back-to-back that has a few really unique shots. These scenes play out across the long corridor in the middle of Redwood while also incorporating the verticality of the city. There is always something to look at while the guns are going off.

Most of the final scenes punctuate emotional moments of revenge and justice. One entirely hand-to-hand fight scene takes place in a textile shop that is full of literal tapestries that juxtapose the wild violence taking place around them. It rules.

This movie is not without its flaws. For anyone who was looking for a well-told story, you are out of luck. The style really is the star of this movie. There are sections of the movie that seem unfinished which is weird considering I think the movie is about 30 minutes too long (please bring back the 90 minute movie). The use of digital blood is disappointing although it does look better in this movie than in most. Also, there is a surprising lack of Idris Elba scenes.

Near the end of the movie, I got a sense that this was a passing of the torch. A generation of actors who have found their foothold in Hollywood lifting up the next generation. This isn't to say that Idris Elba and Regina King are going anywhere. They are some of our best talents. Not only is Regina King now an accomplished director in her own right, but I wouldn't be surprised if she wins **another** Oscar in her lifetime. Actors like Majors, Beetz, Cyler, etc. have all been doing well for themselves but are yet to be the household names they deserve to be.

_The Harder They Fall_ rocks a dope soundtrack, a host of great performers, and a stunning visual style. It does wade around in its own design a little too much but that did not stop me from having a blast. This is the first good Netflix movie in a while. I hope Jeymes Samuel gets a few more chances to direct features because I am excited at what he might try next.
