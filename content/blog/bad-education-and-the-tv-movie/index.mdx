One of the **many** casualties of the streaming era is the TV movie. Some of these movies would air on large networks, cable channels, and who could forget the slate of Disney Channel Original movies we all grew up on? To escape the micro-budget imposed on these films, the true cream-of-the-crop made for TV movies aired on the premium channels. The most popular of these was and still is HBO. A few times a year HBO would release a movie on air and you would get occasional reruns throughout the year. Once HBO Go/Now/Max launched, these movies started to feel like any other streaming movie. Eventually there wasn't a divide between movies like Netflix's _The Kissing Booth 3_ and HBO's _Bad Education_. Meanwhile, there is a world of difference in quality. While Netflix (and other streaming content providers) impose rules on style, format, and even shot framing on their directors, HBO gives directors a chance to make something that is actually good. _Bad Education_ was not heavily promoted by HBO despite its stacked cast and its promising young director. The fact that it took a person like me who loves this type of movie a full year to watch it shows a fault in the system.

_Bad Education_ fits the prototypical mold that HBO movies follow. It is a true story about a scandal or controversial individual. This is a trend for a lot of made-for-television movies. It is an easy way to hook people in and not rely on a huge budget. "Did you hear that thing we saw on the news is now a movie with a bunch of hot people in it?" is probably the line that sells all these movies to producers. I am admittedly a bit ignorant of the actual quality of these movies because I have not seen very many of them. I hear them talked about and even see them get parodied but rarely does one really interest me enough to reach out for that play button. When I heard (over two years after its initial release) that _Bad Education_ was directed by Cory Finley whom I love from his work on 2017's _Thoroughbreds_ and stars one of my favorite on-the-rise actresses in Geraldine Viswanathan, I was already hooked.

_Bad Education_ is about a New York state public school embezzling scandal. The movie relies on compelling characters and a ramping of tension throughout the runtime to let the story play out. Early on we are led to believe that there is only one bad seed in the administrator's office but it is quickly elucidated that the problem goes all the way to the top. Hugh Jackman gets to be unhinged as he tries to cover up the conspiracy. He uses pure rage to shut down the school's accountant and tries to calmly wield his influence on the teenage sleuth who is poised to take him down. As he unravels, you start to see literal cracks in his armor.

Jackman's dedication to the lie and the life he desperately wants to live are what make his performance so special. He gets to play the tender breadwinner and the vulnerable sugar daddy all at once. Even when he eventually ends up in prison, he tries his best to look good because his vanity is all he has left.

Cory Finley deserves a lot of credit for making this movie _look_ so real. The lighting, beige paint, and small spaces paint a very accurate picture of life in the public school system. It helps the viewer feel what pushed these administrators to commit crime. Living in these small boxes and answering to annoying parents would drive anyone to a prison cell. The miraculous thing is that this doesn't happen **more** often (as far as we know).
