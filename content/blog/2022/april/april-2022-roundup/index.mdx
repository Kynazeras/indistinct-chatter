The opening of Daniel Kwan and Daniel Scheinert (the directorial collective known as Daniels)'s 2022 masterpiece _Everything Everywhere All at Once_ is a constantly escalating and stress-inducing thrill ride in which the main character is doing.... laundry and taxes. This sequence sees Michelle Yeoh's Evelyn Wang navigating the hectic life of an immigrant business owner. In her eyes, the world is on her shoulders and everyone close to her is a nuisance. She feels that her husband is ineffectual so she has to be the strong-hand in the family. She refuses to understand her daughter's lesbian relationship. And she respects her father more than he deserves. Pretty early on Evelyn finds out that she is in fact at the center of this universe and many others.

Everything Everywhere All at Once sets out to tell a sprawling story of a broken family. It accomplishes this task by way of the multiverse. That word gets thrown around a lot these days thanks to certain monolithic media franchises but rarely is the concept executed with real imagination. Daniels is able to get us on board quickly by using Michelle Yeoh’s star power as a secret weapon.

Through all the zany and relatable worlds that we see, there is an really touching emotional core to the movie about becoming a better person. Yeoh is obviously phenomenal alongside Ke Huy Quan who has taken a long hiatus from the spotlight after his childhood roles in _Indiana Jones_ and _The Goonies_. Quan is able to switch from amiable to suave to ass-kicking to athletic and all the way back. I hope this sparks some kind of Quan-aissance and we start seeing him more. The breakout performance comes from Stephanie Hsu. She takes her comedy chops to a new level while adding on some deeply emotional sauce. As the movie points out, she is an echo of Evelyn for better or worse. She has just as much internal struggle but with more awareness of those around her.

_Everything Everywhere All at Once_ is the reason we make movies. The worlds are vivid even when we only get a glimpse. They are outlandish but believable. They can get a whole theater to burst out in laughter or tears together. I wanted to get out of my seat and pump my fist at the emotional climax. In an era of overly-stuffed blockbusters that cut and paste their look and feel from one another, Daniels and crew managed to turn in something truly unique. This is the definition of an instant classic.

**Stray Observations**

- I loved the old school martial arts choreography with the chalk dust flying on each hit.
- The aforementioned opening reminds me a lot of 2019's _Uncut Gems_. It never stops moving and you have to keep up in order to get a read on the characters and their struggles.
- I saw it twice in theaters and had the same general experience with the crowd: They were hesitant to laugh or enjoy the movie up until the IRS scene. After that they were on board and having a blast. What a picture.

Robert Egger's latest work is his most sprawling yet. He was given a budget that some would deem too large but I think the sky is the limit. Eggers crafts a Norse revenge myth by way of John Wick. The kills are visceral and the _vibes_ are cold and muddy. Hollywood has always had a real problem utilizing Alexander Skaarsgard but he finally found the role that was made for him. His impressive physique combined with his steely demeanor make his character feel organic to the world. Anya Taylor Joy continues to be in projects I love. _The Northman_ is metal as hell all the way to the fiery end.

**Stray Observations**

- The crowd react to _that_ scene was priceless
- I know this is a small nitpick, but I would love it if we got to more 40 year old actors with 40 year old love interests. I am getting bored of all the female romantic roles being cast 20+ years younger than the man.

This movie is _way_ better than the trailer would have you believe. This is a prime example of why modern trailers are so bad in the first place. A large amount of the plot was given away and a few action set pieces were spoiled. Luckily, I don't see a Michael Bay movie for the plot. I am here for his high-octane directorial style, nihilistic political point-of-view, and some sweet drone photography. He really delivers on that last point. _Ambulance_ has some of the most engaging drone shots I've seen in a while. It is overlong and a little ignorant but when it hits, it _really_ hits. It will probably be no fun to watch at home so catch it in a theater if it's still running!

--- In 2020, I saw two movies the week before COVID closed theaters for over a year:
_The Way Back_ and _Sonic the Hedgehog_. That anecdote has nothing to do with the
review, but I will never let myself forget what a weird time that was. _Sonic the
Hedgehog 2_ delivered on every expectation I had. It was fun enough, a little silly,
and it gave a lot of room for Ben Schwartz to shine. There isn't much to say about
this movie other than it is way too long but still solid as far as video game adaptations
go.

**Stray Observations**

- There was so much time dedicated to the human characters at the Hawaii wedding. They were straight up doing mid 2000s style humor and even had time for a shirtless volley ball game. It was fun enough but taking it out would reduce the runtime to something more manageable.
- Idris Elba had a ton of fun in the final scene. He was genuinely hilarious.

---

# Wrap up

April was a fun month at the movies. _Everything Everywhere all at Once_ and _The Northman_ are frontrunners for best movie of the year. _Ambulance_ was a fun theater spectacle. And \_Sonic the Hedgehog 2 was a fun-enough family film. I am ready for the onslaught of summer movies. There are a ton of tent pole films opening alongside director-driven features so there should be something for everyone.
