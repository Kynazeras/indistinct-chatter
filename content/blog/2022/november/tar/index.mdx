It’s the year 2022 and we’ve had just about as many takes on “cancel culture” as you can get. Unfortunately most of them come from butt-hurt extremists who just want to be allowed to spew hate with no repercussions. Most of the arguments, in the scope of the entertainment industry, end up with terms like “separate the art from the artist” getting without any real acceptance that art is informed by your own world view. Needless to say, these arguments get heated. Todd Field’s newest film _Tár_ is a long and contemplative look at “cancel culture” and the auteur theory with a **career-best** performance from Cate Blanchett.

_Tár_ is about a classical music composer named, coincidentally, Lydia _Tár_. In the opening scenes we are made immediately aware of a few facts: She is calculated, She seems very smart, and the people around her are not very happy to be there. The viewer is lulled into a false sense of security when we see how intelligent and even sweet Lydia can be.

We follow her through the motions for a bit. She does a prestigious New Yorker interview and chats with a fellow conductor. Along the way we get hints that she has bad blood with a former student. These scenes play out incredibly slow but the amount of information received through dialogue and Blanchett’s face is astonishing. There is a lot going on under the surface with this performance and I couldn’t take my eyes off of her.

Eventually we reach a scene that Sean Fennessey called a “litmus test” on the [Big Picture podcast](https://www.theringer.com/2022/10/28/23428908/tar-movie-of-2022-heres-why-cate-blanchett-todd-field). To put it more simply, it is the “let’s talk about cancel culture” scene. While conducting (no pun intended) a guest lecture at Juilliard, Lydia confronts a student who won’t play music written by noted misogynist Johann Sebastian Bach. Lydia is not happy with his reasoning so she gives a long-winded explanation of why she thinks the students should separate the art from the artist. She ends this tangent by openly insulting the student and he storms out. This is probably the most important scene in the movie because it lets the audience establish their feelings for Lydia _Tár_ on their own. This scene will shape how you feel about the events of the rest of the film. Some people will stand firmly with one side while a lot of viewers, myself included, will find themselves somewhere in the middle. I am never going to defend the heinous actions of influential people but we all have to accept that those people can make things we enjoy. In the film world we have our fair share of people like [Roman Polanski](https://www.wikiwand.com/en/Roman_Polanski#/Sexual_abuse_case), but that doesn’t mean we should refuse to learn lessons from his films.

From here, the movie goes on a slow burn warpath to deconstruct Lydia as an auteur and it does so masterfully. There are sequences that with a few slight tweaks would play well in a horror film and there are even a couple laugh-out-loud scenes that slap you in the face. Lydia _Tár_ treats accusations made against her the way any narcissist would and the outcomes give her a rude awakening that lead to violent outbursts and emotional undoing.

The entire time, Todd Field has his eye on the prize. The entire time I was thinking that this is the “anti-TikTok” movie because he really allows each scene to play out at its own pace. The intricacies of human conversation are weaved into the fabric of the film. This makes each scene feel authentic and open. We are not merely subjected to one opinion about the collapse of an icon. We get to see the collapse through her eyes and see all the side effects. The level of collaboration between Blanchett’s powerhouse performance and Todd Field’s careful hand lead to a really transcendent film. I left the theater floored.

As much as I loved _Tár_, I think it will be even better on a second viewing. There are nuances to the performances and sequence of events that can only be amplified upon revisiting. _Tár_ clocks in at 2 hours and 38 minutes but somehow I wanted it to be longer. The final shot is maybe the funniest prestige I’ve seen in a film of this caliber in my life. I highly recommend _Tár_ to anyone who wants a movie that is powerful and doesn’t feel the need to hold your hand the whole time.
