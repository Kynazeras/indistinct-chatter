_Babylon_ is a film released in 2022 directed by Damien Chazelle. Expectations were high for film fans going into this movie because Chazelle’s resume is stacked. His 2014 film _Whiplash_ is one of the most fleshed-out debuts in recent memory (and is also maybe my favorite movie of all time) and in 2016 he wowed all of us with a modern musical that was equal parts decadent and longing in _La La Land_. With his pedigree in tow, he decided to cash in his blank check on a movie about the “golden age of cinema” and the end of the silent era. The film hit production delays in the wake of COVID-19 but speculation never died down. People were ready to see what was next for one of our great contemporary filmmakers.

When the film was released in December of 2022… The crowd went mild. People were split on the movie. Some thought it was an overstuffed mish-mash of Hollywood nonsense and others saw it as a strong entry in Chazelle’s filmography that was just not what people want to watch. As much as I wish this weren’t the case, most audiences don’t want big send-ups to old timey Hollywood. Plus,we had already seen a few directors do the “movies that are about how good movies are” in that same year. By the time I saw it in theaters, the discourse had become non-existant. The film did not do well at the Oscars and it was mostly glossed over in year end lists.

That is a sad fate for a movie that I think is **excellent**. Five stars. A movie whose legacy I sincerely believe will grow as time goes on. I never had time to review the movie but I do have a few major observations that I want to discuss. On the surface, Babylon is about Hollywood and the lives it leaves in its wake whenever times change. Bubbling underneath is a meta-textual narrative about the modern Hollywood system, the streaming era, and Chazelle’s career as a whole. Today I would like to (and I am very proud of this one) peel back the layers of the Babyl-onion.

## Hollywood in the 2020s

There is a headline-worthy fallacy in the modern movie landscape. Anytime a movie longer than 130 minutes is released, people love to complain about the runtimes. However, at the end of the year the highest grossing movie is always over 130 minutes. Of course the movies that get a pass are franchise blockbusters that are too big for their own good and get carried at the box office by FOMO. When a long movie outside of that mold is released, people can’t seem to stand it. Babylon was a victim of this discourse online. I feel like I read more comments about the runtime than the actual quality.

Babylon’s runtime is a feature, not a bug. At times when the movie seems like it is going to drag on, we see the characters stretch themselves. They start to squeeze every last molecule of fame they can. They bend themselves to fit a new mold. Jack Conrad is laughed at by a packed house and has to take pity parts in shit films. Nellie LaRoy fails to be a “modern lady” so she loses everything on drugs and gambling. Sidney Palmer has to disgrace himself just to make his last paycheck. As the characters cling to an industry that is passing them by, the film clings on to the viewer, begging us to care.

I watched Babylon for the second time one week into the combined SAG-AFTRA + WGA strike and it definitely hit different this time around. The characters are going through an existential crisis. They are worried that their time and effort is about to be discounted in favor of the new shiny toy. Meanwhile, real world actors and writers are picketing studios for the same reason. I will probably be making a separate post about artificial intelligence and how dangerous it is to our cultural identity (and overall well being) so suffice it to say that I am not a fan and I fully support the strikes.

## The Streaming Era

In 2010, I got a Netflix account. That same year I started reviewing movies as a hobby. These two things are closely coupled. As a teenager in the rural south, I didn’t have a lot of access to movies outside of rentals and the blockbusters that made it to our theaters. The only time I got to see good movies was when my brother would bootleg something like _Fight Club_ or _Goodfellas_. For that reason, I didn’t have a lot of real opinions on movies. In 2010, I got to watch a lot more movies. I had a whole library of really good and really bad movies at my fingertips. Immediately, I saw the future. A streaming service like Netflix would house cinema’s history. People from all over would be able to connect through film because there would be no more barriers based on regionality and access. We would all be watching and loving movies.

Fast forward to 2023, and that same invention that got me into movies is now killing my enjoyment. Films and television shows are spread across a multitude of services making it impossible to recommend anything to anyone. The prices have gotten so ludicrously high that people have gone back to pirating. Worst of all, people don’t want to go to theaters anymore. Excuse the use of anecdotal evidence but ever since getting my first post-college job in 2018 I’ve met several coworkers who openly admit that they just watch Netflix original movies and mostly use their phone the whole time.

What does this have to do with Babylon? In the last few years, streaming platforms have made big bids on movies by Oscar-caliber directors in hopes of winning and plastering the accolades all over their landing page. On my second viewing, I had a terrifying thought: “what if this thing just got dumped on Apple Tv+ in December?” and that hypothetical almost ruined my whole day. The theatrical release is one of the only elements of auteur-driven features that can keep them alive in the modern discourse. Without it, Babylon would have been dead on arrival.

## Chazelle going forward

Damien Chazelle knew what was going to happen to this movie. The modern cinema landscape is not for the Babylon’s of this world. This was a blank check film. He has garnered enough studio success and accolades with his past few features to earn a shot at this. He wanted to make his loud, bombastic, sexy, depraved, wild, and heartbreaking movie. He knew that he had to leave everything on the screen. That’s another reason the movie is so long. Chazelle feels unsure if he will ever get this shot again. He wants to keep the movie going for as long as they will let him. For all we know, the financial failure of this movie could mean the end of his career. Or worse, it could mean that he has to direct a Disney+ Ki-Adi-Mundi show.

Obviously I hope he gets a better fate. I hope that smaller studios give him mid-range budgets to do weird and interesting stuff. I hope he never releases a movie directly to streaming. I hope he gets to make another modern musical and gets to cast Ryan Gosling again. I hope we get to see another ending like _Whiplash_.

---

The good news is that it's not all doom and gloom for the movies. I started writing this post a week before the "Barbenheimer" craze of the Summer and I have real egg on my face. The box office success of both _Barbie_ and _Oppenheimer_ actually trickled down into mid-budget films and the theaters had their best month in a very long time. I don't think that their problems are solved but they are getting assisstance from all the streaming services raising their prices and cracking down on password sharing. Once the dual strikes are solved and people can make movies happily, we might be in for quite an expansion in theater culture.
