2022 ended up being a great year for movies in my opinion. While I lost a lot of hope in the industry as a whole between huge mergers, project cancellations, and overall bloat of big budget movies that take no risks, I gained just as much watching some of the movies in this list (and quite a few that didn't make the cut). The landscape has been in a free-fall for the past few years but interesting projects are still being made and there is an audience for them (despite some real "_under-performers_"). This list is not really in order aside from maybe the top 5. On any given day I can fight for one of these movies as the best of the year. I had a great time in the theater and I hope 2023 can be just as bright.

## 10.

I have a lot of love for Indian cinema. The cinematic language is very different from ours and that makes for some jarring watching experiences. The movies are very much rooted in persona, a love of music, and sometimes strict adherence to the status quo. There are quite a few Indian movies I love, but none of them come close to the experience of watching _RRR_. The action scenes are **bigger**, the music is **louder**, and the story is absolutely **wild**. There are so many movies today that are way too long and don't understand what the fun part of the film is. That is not the case with _RRR_. It is as fun a 3-hour commitment as you will ever make. Studios like Marvel and Warner Brothers are _trying_ to make movies as relevant as this one and they all fail because they have forgotten how to have fun. Watch _RRR_ if you haven't. It is rad.

## 8.

<MovieCard
  title="The Fabelmans"
  starRating={4}
  director="Steven Spielberg"
  actors={["Gabriel LaBelle", "Michelle Williams", "Paul Dano", "Seth Rogen"]}
  posterLink="https://www.themoviedb.org/t/p/w600_and_h900_bestv2/xId9zoiv5WPQOuxqykUDrlpmrUz.jpg"
/>

Spielberg is one of those "when is he bad?" guys. I don't **always** like his movies but they are always at least pretty good from a directing stand point. _The Fabelmans_ is a movie about why that is true. I'm not kidding. There is a scene that boils down to one character angrily asking Steven Spielberg "Why are you so good at making movies?!" to which Steven replies "I really don't know". Also, it is unironically the best scene in the film. _The Fabelmans_ is not for everyone but it is a complete knock out if you are on board. There are so many great vignettes and a lot of heart in the performances. Once it is over and you run the meta-textual narrative in your head, you realize how great Spielberg has to be to have committed this to film.

There is a lot of _Nope_ hate out in the wild. It has to be the movie I argued about the most this year. It is divisive and that is what great movies need to be. We live in a time of movies that are made my committee to please everyone and in reality they please no one. They make money but no one remembers them after they leave the theater. Like it or not you remembered _Nope_. People a lot smarter than me have praised the film already so I will leave it at that.

Ruben Östlund makes films about social dynamics and the politics of relationships. In _Triangle of Sadness_ he tackles the hierarchy amongst the wealthy and the working class. Beautiful people with little regard of blue collar workers get a reality check when they no longer have modern conveniences. It is funny, gross, and incisive. I had my loudest laugh of the year during a scene that was making others wretch. _Triangle of Sadness_ was truly refreshing in the way that it handled its script and casting. I am looking to watch the rest of Östlund's filmography soon.

## 5.

Damien Chazelle made (in my opinion) the greatest movie of all time back in 2014 with _Whiplash_. Everything since then has been a victory lap. His biggest success came with his most vibrant film: 2016's _La La Land_. His biggest failure came this year with his most interesting movie: _Babylon_.

Why did it fail? I don't know. Maybe because it is a high budget movie about the silent film era released the same week as a new _Avatar_ movie. Maybe because it was made specifically for _dang-ass freaks_ like **ME** and not for most normal audience members. It could be anything really.

_Babylon_ is about the ending of the silent film era and how it affected the stars who thrived in that system. It features a career highlight performance from Margot Robbie who is just electric against the drug and jazz-fueled environment of old Hollywood. Diego Calva is a breakout star who gives a nuanced performance from beginning to end. Brad Pitt is.... well Brad Pitt. His casting fits like a glove and sometimes I forget he is even acting. The cast is littered with great character actors and smaller performances that keep you wrapped up for over 3 hours.

The run time is the elephant (violently shitting) in the room. The first complaint I heard was "it is too long and it drags at the end". Funnily enough, I agree. However I believe the length is a feature, not a bug. This film is about people clinging to the last vestiges of their fame. They are attempting to stay alive and relevant for as long as they can. They don't want to let go and neither does Chazelle. The writing was on the wall that this movie wouldn't be a hit. Chazelle is not a household name at this point so his blank checks are not guaranteed clears. **Chazelle wants this to last**. He is putting every last second that he can directly on screen. By the end, looking at Manny was like looking in a mirror. I am a sucker for movies about how great movies are and this works so damn well.

_Decision to Leave_ is the reason why I struggle to write a review for every movie that I watch. I tried for a whole week to put something together and it all fell flat. Park Chan-wook is a masterful filmmaker with a steady hand. The scenery in this movie looks like it was ripped from the pages of a story book. The plot is _a little_ overly complicated but the ending is oh so sweet. You are left with so much imagery and pieces to the puzzle that the final decision just knocks you out. I would nominate the scene where the two main characters meet for the first time as "Scene of the Year".

Rian Johnson really gets me. I am a nerd who likes a good mystery and he has always delivered. _Glass Onion_ is par for the course in his filmography. He has crafted yet another big cast murder mystery that is funny and sincere. His jabs at tech billionaires and honestly impressive knowledge of internet culture help him put Benoit Blanc into a new situation while maintaining the first movie's high level of charm. The colors are bright and the performances are just so sweet. _Glass Onion_ should have been seen by more people in theaters. It was clearly crafted for that experience and I wish Netflix would understand that.

## 1.

_EEAAO_ came out pretty early in the year to make it this far. Most of the time, first half of the year front-runners are overshadowed by the award season hitters but that is not the case here. Upon first viewing, I knew this film was special and on repeat viewings it only solidifies itself more and more as an instant classic. From the amazing career revival of Ke Huy Quan to the on screen homages to classic international cinema and internet culture, I am still amazed everything comes together the way it does. I really hope Daniels take the lessons they learned from this movie and become as the new age of "finger on the pulse" directors. And if they aren't able to do it then I hope this movie inspires others.

---

There were some incredibly tough decisions while making this list. The omission of _Banshees of Inisherin_, _Three Thousand Years of Longing_, and _X_ saddens me but sacrifices have to be made to cut the list down to 10. I won't begin to hazard a guess as to what 2023 has in store but I hope some of our top directors and a slew of up-and-comers can deliver another great list of films.
