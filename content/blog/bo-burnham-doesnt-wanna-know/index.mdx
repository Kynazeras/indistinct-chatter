For anyone who is unaware, Bo Burnham is one of my biggest influences in life. His first few albums were very formative for me and my sense of humor. I’ve seen him live every time he toured after I turned 18. I have watched/listened to pretty much everything that has his name on it. He is one of the few people who I call a “genius” without any exaggeration. For a decade he has been making stuff that hits me right where it matters (the heart and the funny bone). He isn’t afraid to grow and change. He started out making “offensive” songs on YouTube before it was really a platform for launching stars. Since then he has gotten more and more introspective and just can’t seem to miss. In 2018 he made his directorial debut with [_Eighth Grade_](https://www.imdb.com/title/tt7014006/?ref_=nv_sr_srsg_0) and it was one of the best movies that year. All of that goes to say: I really respect Bo Burnham’s work.

In 2021, he had his most successful release to date. His Netflix special [_Inside_](https://www.imdb.com/title/tt14544192/?ref_=tt_ch) got tons of people watching and listening to his thoughts and jokes. As a long time fan, I loved seeing people discover him and become fans themselves. It was a long time coming for a guy as talented as him. I went as far as to rank his special as my [fourth favorite **film** of that year](https://letterboxd.com/parttimepretzel/list/2021/) (it was feature length so it counts). The album created a lot of hits. Both “Bezos” songs got huge on TikTok. “White Woman’s Instagram” was able to call out a whole generation of social media users. “All eyes on me” was able to sound like a club banger while also being depressing. What a combination. One song that didn’t get a lot of love, “Don’t Wanna Know”, is my very favorite from the collection. I don’t love “Don’t Wanna Know” because I can put it on repeat, but because it illustrates my issue with modern movies and the “Content” era.

I have a pretty doom-and-gloom outlook on our current era of movies and television. I don’t think that **EVERY** show and film coming out is necessarily bad (there are several movies I’ve really liked this year in fact) but there has been a serious downturn in the average quality of movies. You can’t judge a time period on how good its best movies are but how good its average movies are. Lately, “average” movies have been relegated to streaming and get hamstrung creatively. A lot of movies are designed to be “watched” while you are on your phone. If you actually pay attention to them, you realize they are vapid and flavorless. Sadly, this means that even the good stuff on streaming is treated the same way. Until I watched _Inside_, I only thought about this from a fan’s perspective. I hated seeing people ignore important movies in favor of whatever came out on Netflix that week. I never thought about how hard it is on the creators of good stuff. “Don’t Wanna Know” is all about that feeling and despite it’s minute and a half runtime, it says so much.

> How are you feeling?
> Do you like the show?
> Are you tired of it?
> Never mind, I don’t wanna know
> Are you finding it boring?
> Too fast? Too slow?
> I’m asking, but don’t answer
> ‘Cause I don’t wanna know

“Don’t Wanna Know” serves as a segue/interlude. It is a break from the biting commentary or wall-to-wall jokes. Or so you think. Bo is checking in on the viewer. He asks questions but doesn’t want answers because they are potentially disheartening. He knows there is a non-zero chance that someone watching this special is on their phone and mostly tuned out of what he is saying.

> Is there anyone out there?
> Or am I all alone?
> It wouldn’t make a difference
> Still, I don’t wanna know

Bo pontificates on the idea that maybe he is alone. Maybe he wrote, recorded, directed, and edited this piece of art but no one is even watching. Maybe he is telling jokes to the ether. Maybe his songs are floating endlessly with no one to appreciate or criticize what they see.

I don’t know about you, but the idea of that **FUCKING SUCKS**. This is probably not a unique thought either. Bo may have turned it into a catchy tune but this thought is in the heads of so many creatives. As we slowly start to classify movies, television, podcasts, and music as “content” we are losing what makes them all so special. Artists are never sure how people will consume what they make. You can’t even lay hope in theater audiences because people will just openly use their phone there too. The guy sitting next to me in the theater for [Zola](https://www.imdb.com/title/tt5439812/) was Snapchatting the whole time and even answered a phone call without excusing himself. I doubt that was the intended viewing experience.

I wish I could end this by saying: "But there is a bright side and things are getting better"; but I don’t know what will happen next. Our blockbusters feel lifeless. Our television shows are losing the plot (a lot of television should probably have been a feature film but no one wanted to fund it). Every single person has a podcast and a line of NFTs. People are forgetting what it means to make art. However, I am starting to be more optimistic. Movies like Ti West’s _X_ are shocking people back into theaters. Shows like [_Succession_](https://www.imdb.com/title/tt7660850/?ref_=fn_al_tt_1) and [_Invincible_](https://www.imdb.com/title/tt6741278/?ref_=nv_sr_srsg_0) are righting the “prestige” ship. There are very bright spots out there. They are a little harder to find but they deserve your (full and undivided) attention nonetheless.

I’d ask you if you read this whole article, _but I don’t wanna know._

**DISCLAIMER:** I am not saying that ALL Netflix/Hulu/HBO Max etc. movies are bad. Far from it. Every one of those services has a few banner movies each year that deserve the hype. The issue is the average.
