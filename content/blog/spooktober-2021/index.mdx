There are so many reasons to be a fan of October. Nature lovers get to see the leaves turn. Coffee lovers get a load of seasonal drinks. And for us movie lovers, we get to indulge in Spooktober movie marathons. The best part is that you don’t even have to be a fan of horror. There are tons of great October movies that just embrace the fun parts of Halloween without high intensity scares. I am a fan of both so I’ve been having a blast revisiting favorites as well as finding some gems.

This year I spent time with John Carpenter’s filmography because of my love for the Blank Check podcast. I’ve always been a fan of his work so the podcast covering him was as good an excuse as any to dive right in. I also spent a fair amount of time watching horror television for the first time. I had not watched any horror shows but this year’s release of Midnight Mass was a good kickstarter to explore the medium. I have very much enjoyed the Mike Flanagan shows on Netflix (but we will talk more about that later)

Since the month is a little over half way done, I thought it was time to do a run down of my favorite Spooktober 2021 watches and talk about what is still on the list

Coherence is certainly my favorite hidden gem from this year. The low runtime and budget are able to take a high concept and make it as easy to grasp as possible. This is mostly achieved by letting the viewer draw their own conclusions about how deep the rabbit hole can go while allowing the main character to fight(quite literally) for a happy ending.

What's even more interesting is the story of [how this film got made](<https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Coherence_(film)#Production>). Instead of writing a full script, the movie's dialog and machinations were mostly made up on the spot. The actors were given short descriptions of their characters and the set pieces that kept the film moving. Other than that, it all came from them. The actors weren't even familiar with each other so the relationships were built over the five day shooting period. I think that the lean nature of the production kept the movie from spiraling into a convoluted mess(unlike 2020's disappointing outing from Christopher Nolan, Tenet)

There isn't much to say about John Carpenter's masterpiece The Thing other than this: it is a perfect movie. I am a sucker for practical effects and they are the star of this movie. Every creepy crawler and human mutilation is done with the kind of care and attention that modern Hollywood should take note of.

As a side note, I watched this movie in 4K for the first time and I was blown away. The color blue has never looked as good in a movie. The characters are bathed in the light while the snowy setting makes even the darker scenes easy to follow. I remember watching a movie on standard Blu-ray for the first time and thinking it couldn't get any better than this. I stand corrected.

Also, It wasn't until I stared at Kurt Russell's young face that I connected the dots that he is Wyatt Russell father. That makes sense because Wyatt Russell is one of my favorite actors right now. If you haven't seen Lodge 49, you should check it out.

Here is a John Carpenter movie that I have some complicated feelings about. Most of my favorite critics give this movie high marks and I see why. Carpenter is able to craft real characters and sense of urgency in such a tight runtime. You have very little room to breathe as a viewer and it creates the perfect atmosphere for the picture. Combine that with great performances and a truly weird gang of wildly large numbers and equally wild rituals as the antagonist and what you get a solidly entertaining movie that moves.

However, watching this movie in 2021 made me feel weird. Considering [how vocal Carpenter about his politics](https://www.nme.com/features/film-interviews/john-carpenter-interview-horror-2872541), I just expected a little more criticism of the police. There were moments, like when one police cruiser won't do a thorough search of an area because it doesn't look like there is a crime taking place, where I can see the cracks in the system. Plus, it was made in a time where open criticism of the police might not fly.

As I mentioned earlier, horror television became a big part of my October watchlist this year. I had never seen any of the Mike Flanagan Netflix shows and now I have finished 2 out of 3. I started with his most recent project, Midnight Mass. I knew very little of his style going in. I had only seen Doctor Sleep in the past and I was not a huge fan (although now might be the time to give it a second chance). Right away Midnight Mass made an impression on me. The dreary lighting and remote setting built a distinct atmosphere. The small town with one primary religion was a particularly smart decision because viewers like myself can heavily relate to that situation.

What made the show special to me is what others seem to malign it for: the **long** scenes of two people talking. Some have, less than lovingly, renamed the show _Midnight Monologues_. Sadly it is one of those "You either love it or hate it" scenarios. I found it very effective. The hardest part about doing horror is getting the characters right and making them feel real. Film gets the benefit of brevity where we can forgive some underwritten dialog if the scares deliver but television offers no such wiggle room. Great television lives and dies by the characters. Midnight Mass has a slew of rich characters who all get at least one moment to shine. There are some particularly great conversations in the show and it made me want to get to know the people better.

At this point, Flanagan had won me over and I decided to watch the rest of his television endeavors. I was sure that none of them would be able to impress me as much as Midnight Mass. Until...

The Haunting of Hill House is honestly one of the best things Netflix has ever made. It is in the same pantheon as Bojack Horseman and American Vandal. Everything I loved about Midnight Mass is here but bigger. Character-driven horror does not get better than this.

There are tons of movies and shows that attempt a broken chronology. While some are good, most have some missteps when it comes to pacing. Hill House is a shining example of how to pull it off. There are episodes that swing wildly between distant past, recent past, and present without any of the associated whiplash. I never felt like I needed to take a pause to catch up on what was going on because the storytelling is **on point**. On top of that is great performances (including many talented child actors) and even some [technically marvelous](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=n8gJ81M39Ig) episodes.

Also, I am pretty sure Hill House has the best jump scare of all time. Jump scares usually lie somewhere between cheap trick to get a reaction and well timed payoff to built up tension but this one is special. There is a jump scare in the back half of the show that hits during a highly emotional scene and leads to two characters finally being able to reconcile. If you know of a better jump scare, please [let me know](https://twitter.com/PartTimePretzel)
