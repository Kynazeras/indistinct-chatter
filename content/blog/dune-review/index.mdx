# Coming from the book

This section is going to serve as a bit of a disclaimer. There is no way I will be able to divorce this movie from its source material. I have read the original _Dune_ novel several times since I was sixteen years old. It has shaped a lot of my opinions on science fiction. I have also considered it to be un-adaptable. The source book is dense and does very little hand-holding. A gaggle of in-universe jargon is thrown at you from page one and you are meant to connect the dots. Luckily the prose and world building are so rich that it is easy to get up to speed once you commit. This is why I am not a fan of David Lynch's interpretation from the 80's. I know a lot of people have a soft spot in their heart for that movie but for me it just isn't Dune.

Despite my trepidation to anyone else trying to adapt the book, I got chills when I heard that Denis Villeneuve would be at the helm this time. Villeneuve already did the impossible in 2017 by making a truly phenomenal sequel to Bladerunner. He rapidly became one of my favorite working directors and I was ready for him to bring his uncompromising artistic vision to this work that I hold near and dear.

# What I liked

## The Scale

The Scale is the immediate standout of the film. People and landscapes are dwarfed by gigantic ships and gargantuan worms. You are meant to feel small in the vast universe of Dune. The intergalactic politics and great family feuds impress on the viewer a sense of urgency and unrest in all the characters. Their interactions and ceremonies are larger than life. The first ceremony we see is in front of one of the aforementioned large ships with a crowd of faces in intricate masks of varying styles. We know little of their cultures and lifestyles but we know one thing: it is so much bigger than what is on screen. I even felt small when the onscreen text let us know that we are in the year 10000. I realized that sitting in the theater in 2021 means I am essentially in the dark ages.

## The Sound

Sound design kind of goes hand-in-hand with the above. This movie has the best in-theater sound I have heard in a very long time. The score swells when ships are landing and taking off. The sand storms rip and tear at you, instilling the same fear that the characters are feeling at the moment. _The Voice_ has a sound effect that is almost sounds like it belongs in a horror movie. Whether it is expected or abrupt, that sound cuts right through a scene.

## The Performances

There was little doubt that the all star cast of the movie would be able to deliver. As much as I love the casting of Timothée Chalamet and believe he is a good choice for Paul Atreides, I have to shine the spotlight on Rebecca Ferguson and Oscar Isaac. Ferguson is _exactly_ what I wanted from Lady Jessica. You could see how much she knew about the prophecies and how worried she was about Paul. The scene where she is outside of the room while the Reverend Mother tests Paul is gut wrenching. The is able to shape the concerned mother archetype around the world of _Dune_ to create something special.

While Ferguson delivered exactly what I wanted, Oscar Isaac delivered what I never knew I needed. Isaac brought a level of passion and sincerity to Leto that gives the character new meaning. He has a conversation with Paul early on in the movie that was so sincere. On my next re-read, I am going to see the character in a new light

## The Production Design

Everyone has their own image of _Dune_ that they've built up over years. [Some interpretations](https://www.muddycolors.com/2020/03/moebius-dune/) embrace the truly open nature of the text. Villeneuve's vision is incredibly modern. Everything from the armor of the infantry to the interior design is stark concrete. Rooms are grey and clothing is solid dark colors. Everything fits together and the craft behind it all deserves a round of applause.

# What I didn't like

## The Limitations

Yes I know this is such a "I read the book, and actually..." kind of complaint. I do not hold this against the movie at all. My rating is based off the quality, not my expectations. In the interest of runtime, the stories belonging to characters like Thurfil Hawat, Pitir De Vries, and Feyd Rautha were basically omitted. The book is dense and fitting everything in would be difficult. My main hope is that [Stephen McKinley Henderson](https://www.imdb.com/name/nm0376610/) gets more work now that he is working with big time directors.

## The Sound Mix

While the overall sound was phenomenal (and reason enough to see this thing in a theater), there were a few times when the mix caused the dialog to get washed away. For me, this wasn't a huge problem because I had a good idea of what the characters were talking about. For newcomers I can see this being a big issue.

# Final Thoughts

Overall I would say that Villeneuve has done the impossible again. He took _Dune_ and made it into an on-screen Sci-Fi epic. He packed it wall-to-wall with interesting sets and costumes and he hired the right actors to take the work seriously. I sincerely hope that we will get part two. Hollywood already suppresses so much creativity in the pursuit of profit. I hope this movie doesn't fall victim. So to keep that dream alive, I am going to quote [letterboxd user SilentDawn](https://letterboxd.com/silentdawn/) to say "see this fucker on the biggest, loudest screen you can find"
